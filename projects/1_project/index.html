Potential impact of 1.5 ¬∞C and 2 ¬∞C global warming on consecutive dry and wet days over West Africa

Analysis of how 1.5¬∞C and 2¬∞C warming levels affect dry and wet spell extremes (CDD and CWD) over West Africa using CORDEX RCMs, with significant implications for regional agriculture.

This project page details the findings of the research published in Environmental Research Letters (2018), which investigates the critical climate risks faced by West Africa under the Paris Agreement warming targets. The study uses a multi-model ensemble of 25 Regional Climate Model (RCM) simulations from the Coordinated Regional Downscaling Experiment (CORDEX) to assess the impact on seasonal precipitation extremes.

The primary focus was on two key extreme precipitation and seasonal drought indicators: the Maximum Number of Consecutive Dry Days (CDD) and the Maximum Number of Consecutive Wet Days (CWD), analyzed at both the +1.5 ¬∞C and +2 ¬∞C Global Warming Levels (GWLs) above pre-industrial levels.

The results show a noticeable, robust response to enhanced warming, with an overall reduction in mean rainfall across the region. Specifically, the models project the following:

  • Consecutive Dry Days (CDD) will increase over the Guinea Coast, indicating a tendency toward longer and more intense dry spells.
  • Consecutive Wet Days (CWD) will decrease, suggesting shorter rainy periods.

More than 80% of the ensemble members agree on the sign of these changes. These projected shifts are critical, as they directly influence the already fragile ecosystems and rain-fed agriculture in West Africa, necessitating urgent adaptation and mitigation strategies.
